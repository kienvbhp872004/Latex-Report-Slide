3.1. Multi-scale decomposition based methods 
In the transform domain, the typical flow diagram of image fu- 
sion based on multi-scale decomposition (MSD) is illustrated in 
Fig. 3 . Source images are first decomposed into multi-scale co- 
efficients using a decomposition transform, then coefficients are 
merged according to some fusion rules, and finally the inverse of 
the transform is applied to reconstruct the fused image [38] . It is 
important to notice that the multi-scale scheme can be extended 
to more source images. Hence, advantageous MSD properties made 
them a revolutionary tools due to their ability to extract spatial 
structures over different scales. Mainly, the main strength lies in 
the fusion strategy which depends on the activity level measure- 
ment of coefficients, the consistency verification, the correlation of 
adjacent pixels and the coefficient grouping strategy [39] . 
3.1.1. Pyramid transform 
In the early eighties, the notion of scales was introduced before 
the emergence of wavelets , aiming to localize objects in the image. 
One of the most primitively employed MSD tools was the pyra- 
midal transforms. Hereby, the decomposition process consists of 
successive filtering and down sampling steps, resulting in a set of 
band-pass and low-pass copies of the image that imitate the pyra- 
mid structure. Low frequencies are concentrated in higher levels 
[40] . Decomposition ( Eq. 1 ) and reconstruction ( Eq. (2) ) are given

based methods suffer from blocking artifacts and furnish undesired 
edges. The following transforms have investigated these shortcom-

3.1.2. Wavelet transform 
The first wavelets was developed in 1909 by Alfred Haar , and 
has surpassed the limitation of time-frequency localization. Ini- 
tially, it has been presented as optimal tools to analyze one- 
dimensional signals in 1980, then moved towards 2-D image repre- 
sentations. Mallat et al. [46] reported that properties of the wavelet 
transform (WT) are suitable for particular signal processing appli- 
cations such as compression. Particularly, it has been found effi- 
cient in extracting information details from one image to inject it 
into another based on additions, substitutions or selection strate- 
gies [47] . Often, WT is an extension of the high pass filtering 
idea, and are typically based on functions that are localized in 
both frequency and space. It can be described as a combination of 
two groups of functions; wavelet functions and scaling functions. 
Hence, the wavelet functions are called the mother wavelets. Trans- 
formations of the mother are termed the daughter wavelets which 
should be scaled and translated [47] . A normalization factor is also

The discrete W T (DW T), with capabilities in capturing location 
as well as frequency information, is able to better separate high 
frequencies from low frequencies, but lacks of shift-invariance due 
the down sampling process [49] . The state of the art in MMIF 
based on wavelets was chronologically reviewed in well-ranked 
journals [3,9,47,50] . Pajares et al. [50] reviewed image fusion in 
the wavelet domain and cited three reasons behind its appropri- 
ateness; (1) DWT preserves information through image decomposi- 
tion, (2) coefficients can be appropriately combined to obtain new 
ones that best collect the original information, and (3) it is well 
suited to manage different image resolutions. However, wavelets 
show its drawbacks while representing higher number of direc- 
tional features. It was proven deficient of shift invariance prop- 
erty, along with inability to efficiently represent anisotropic fea- 
tures and sharp transitions like edges [39] . DWT was proposed to 
merge different combinations of multi-modalities such as MRI/CT 
[51] , PET/CT [52] and PET/MRI [13] , etc. 
The first wavelet based image fusion schemes appeared in mid 
nineties to outperform pyramid based methods. The dual tree com- 
plex wavelet transform (DTCWT), nearly shift-invariant and less 
aliasing, has extended the direction support to six [24] . The non- 
subsampled DTCWT has enhanced the phase information, shift- 
invariance and multi-scalability in [19] . The sift-invariance prop- 
erty of the redundant DWT (RDWT) has also provided better fusion 
results in [53] . Furthermore, several extensions of WT were suc- 
cessfully applied MMIF such as, the lifting WT [36] , multi-wavelet 
transform (MWT) which provides more texture information [18] , 
Daubechies complex W T (DCW T) [54] , stationary W T (SW T) [14] , 
morphological wavelet [55] . The Quaternion wavelet transform 
(QWT) has some merits over WT and DCWT through its shift in- 
variant magnitude better for textural information, and its richer 
geometric information given by its phase [56] . Meanwhile, the fu- 
sion process in the wavelet domain can occur in the pixel level and 
also in the mid-level by merging directional features [57] . Recent 
works proposed different combinations to take benefits from dif- 
ferent transforms, including but not limited to, wavelet-contourlet 
[14] , wavelet-curvelet [58] and wavelet-ripplet [59] and wavelet- 
shearlet [60] . The main objective behind these hybrid combinations 
is to overcome limitations of wavelets by selecting more salient di- 
rectional features. In the decision level, DWT has been adopted to- 
gether with self-organizing neural networks as a fusion rule [61] . 
Several feature and decision fusion schemes gave rise to hybrid 
approaches for instance; wavelet/SVM [62] , wavelet/independent 
component analysis (ICA) [63] , Wavelet/fuzzy entropy [64] , and 
wavelet/neuro-fuzzy [65,66] . Different meta-heuristic optimization 
algorithms found applications in this field such as genetic evolu- 
tionary algorithm [67] , and grey wolf optimization [68] . Recently 
in 2020, Bhardwaj et al [69] used an optimized Bayesian modal 
based on genetic algorithm in the wavelet domain in order to fuse 
different MRI modalities (Flair, T1, T2). Hereby, the genetic algo- 
rithm is used to optimize the fusion parameter of Bayesian modal. 
However, they have been reported to be time-consuming due to 
random optimization process. Practically, effectiveness of these hy- 
brid methods across all modalities is a challenging problem since 
being skewed towards quality of the training images that differs 
from one imaging condition to another [9] . 
3.1.3. Ridgelet transform 
Multi-scale geometric decomposition (MGD) was introduced to 
overcome the limitation of directionality by defining some rules 
on the wavelet functions or by proposing specific treatment within 
wavelets. According to the review in [48] , these transforms, termed 
directional or geometrical, can be grouped into two families; adap- 
tive transforms (e.g., bandelet, wedgelet) and non-adaptive trans- 
forms (e.g., curvelet, contourlet). The first family builds an adap- 
tive structure of the image, while the second defines additive 
mathematical rules on wavelets for amelioration. A panorama on 
multi-scale geometric representation theory was developed in [70] . 
Wavelets loose performance in detecting 1D singularities when de- 
composing 2D images using 2D separable basis. However, ridgelet 
transform (RT) outperforms WT in representing 1D singularities, 
mostly linear singularities in images [71] . 
Based on wavelet theory, the ridgelet transform arose from a 
need to find a sparse representation of objects with discontinu- 
ities across straight lines. Developed by Candès in 1998 [72] , it is 
basically a tool for shape and ridge detection in images. 2-D RT of

3.1.5. Contourlet transform 
Besides CVT, another transform named contourlet (CRT) was de- 
veloped in 2005 by Do et al. [84] aiming to capture the intrin- 
sic geometrical features of an image and to enhance the isotropic 
property of wavelets. As a directional multi-resolution transform, 
the decomposition comprises two filtering steps. First, a Laplacian 
pyramid is applied to select point discontinuities, then a local di- 
rectional decomposition is performed through a directional filter 
bank (DBF) to link these discontinuities into a linear structures 
[39,83] . The set of basis oriented along different scales and direc- 
tions made CRT an effective tool to select intrinsic contours rather 
than with curvelets or ridgelets. Fig. 5 .(a) shows the block diagram 
of the CRT. However, due to the down-sampling and up-sampling, 
it is not shift-invariant. The so called non-subsampled contourlet 
transform (NSCT) is a fully shift invariant expansion that has fast 
implementation where Laplacian decomposition and DBF are both 
non-subsampled [85] . The frequency partition in Fig. 5 .(b) depicts 
8 directions (1–4 subbands correspond to the mostly horizontal di- 
rections while 5–8 correspond to the mostly vertical directions). 
For instance, several studies used CRT to preserve local bright- 
ness and reduce distortion in CT/MRI images like in [55,86] . It was 
also performed to add structural information from MRI to func- 
tional information of PET based on local energy and local variance 
[87] . Nevertheless, NSCT was worthy to be investigated due to its 
shift-invariance, multi-scale and multi-direction properties [85] . To 
this end, hybrid schemes were proposed in literature such as NSCT 
with PCA [88] , or Gabor filter bank [86] . Correspondingly, sparse 
representation (SR) was not spared to be joined with NSCT such 
as in [89] using a clustering-based dictionary learning in the NSCT 
domain. In [90] , low-pass bands were merged using SR as an ac- 
tivity level measurement. 
In a “shallow” learning fashion, different extensions of pulse 
coupled neural network (PCNN) were conceptualized to manage 
NSCT sub-images. In [32] , Tian et al. proposed an improved PCNN 
where the linking strength parameter is calculated using the lo- 
cal area singular value decomposition of the structural information 
factor. In [91] , Dai et al. designed an adaptive local visual contrast 
PCNN [91] . Both schemes outperformed WT and NSCT. The orig- 
inal PCNN was adopted in [92] for high-frequency subbands fu- 
sion. Moreover, fuzzy logic has been introduced in order to effi- 
ciently handle uncertainties. It can be seen as feature transform 
operator or a decision operator [93] . The fusion process adopted 
either a neuro-fuzzy model [25] , or a type-2 fuzzy based fusion 
rule [94] . Consequently, the uncertainty modeling achieved good 
visual effects with less degradation. Likewise, the recent study 
in [26] sparsely represented subbands with learned dictionaries 
within low and high sub-images, afterwards PCNN is motivated 
to select coefficients according to the large firing times. However, 
these transforms were recognized to be not very efficient in cap- 
turing geometric singularities in multidimensional signals like im- 
ages due to the isotropic dilation property of wavelets.

The sparsity criterion has been reported to be beneficial for var- 
ious imaging applications, specifically in various image fusion tasks 
such as remote sensing [101] , infrared and visible images [102] and 
multi-focus images [103] . In the medical field, various modalities 
have been fused within the three abstraction levels; for instance, 
PET/MRI of brain tissues in the pixel level [104] , 3D-T1/T2 MRI 
in the feature level [105] , and MRI/CT in the decision level [106] . 
Most existing shearlet-based fusion methods belong to the pixel 
level category. The feature level depends on the fusion rule be- 
tween correlated coefficients which is a challenging task. In par- 
ticular, the highest level is application-dependent and provides in 
certain cases best fusion performance. Sparse representation the- 
ory takes advantages of good anisotropy and directional selectivity. 
Hermessi et al. [66] fused CT and MRI by using neuro-fuzzy in- 
ference in the shearlet domain. Tan et al. [107] trained K-means 
singular value decomposition (K-SVD) with low frequency shearlet 
coefficients to fuse sub-images based on orthogonal matching pur- 
suit (OMP). In [105] , 3D high frequency subbands are merged using 
structure tensor and rank analysis method. Duan et al. evaluated 
their 3D dual tree ST-based approach with different direction and 
scale numbers, and reported that increased direction number (su- 
perior to 48) cannot always result in a better fused image. Earlier, 
Qiu et al. [108] enhanced the sparsity criterion in the shearlet do- 
main through dynamic group sparsity recovery (DGSR) algorithm 
instead of OMP. Besides, fuzzy logic in the shearlet domain was not 
deeply explored in literature. A feature-level volumetric MRI fusion 
framework was introduced in [23] using 3D-ST where type-2 fuzzy 
entropy is performed on high frequency subbands. 
Several other fusion strategies have took advantages of ST in- 
cluding but not restricted to, PCA [109] , SVD [110] , and non- 
negative matrix factorization [15] . In summary, ST-based methods 
better preserve most of the useful information (anatomical and 
functional) and contrast, typically outperforms the above explored 
MGDs. This is due to directional sensitivity where the number of 
orientation at each scale is unlimited. Mainly, many other trans- 
forms have been proposed in medical image fusion, including the 
higher order generalization of CVT termed ripplet-I transform [12] , 
the ripplet-II transform [111] , the grouplet transform [112] , the 
framelet transform [113] , the tetrolet transform [35] , etc. However, 
there is a trade-off between noise and artifacts in the fused re- 
sults and capability to detect spatial details [83] . Indeed, the wide 
range image fusion in the pixel level has not spared the feature de- 
cision levels. In feature-based techniques, MGDs have contributed 
[19,23,109] to improve the fusion performance, but still depends 
on the image quality, which needs careful attention when design- 
ing such pipelines. Noise also affects the fusion quality in the fea- 
ture level. A pre-processing should be applied to remove undesired 
noise and artifacts.
